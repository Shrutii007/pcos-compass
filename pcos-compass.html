🥦 Diet & Nutrition for PCOS

Diet is one of the most powerful tools for managing PCOS — especially for reducing insulin resistance, which affects up to 70% of women with PCOS.

Foods to eat more of:

  • High-fiber vegetables (broccoli, spinach, kale, cauliflower)
  • Lean protein (chicken, fish, eggs, legumes, tofu)
  • Anti-inflammatory foods (berries, olive oil, fatty fish, nuts)
  • Low-glycemic carbs (sweet potato, quinoa, oats, brown rice)
  • Spearmint tea — shown to reduce androgen levels

Foods to reduce:

  • Refined sugars and white carbs (white bread, pastries, sugary drinks)
  • Processed and fried foods
  • Dairy (can trigger inflammation in some women with PCOS)
  • Alcohol — disrupts hormone balance

💊 PCOS Treatment Options

PCOS has no cure, but symptoms are very manageable. Treatment is personalised — what works for one woman may differ for another.

Lifestyle (first line of treatment):

  • Even 5–10% weight loss can restore regular periods
  • Low-glycemic diet reduces insulin and androgen levels
  • Regular exercise — especially strength training

Common medications your doctor may discuss:

  • Metformin — improves insulin sensitivity
  • Birth control pills — regulate periods and reduce androgens
  • Spironolactone — reduces hair growth and acne
  • Clomid or Letrozole — for fertility treatment

Always work with your doctor to find the right plan for your body.

🧠 Mental Health & PCOS

Women with PCOS are significantly more likely to experience anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem — and it's not just "in your head." Hormonal imbalances directly affect brain chemistry and mood.

  • High androgens and insulin resistance can affect serotonin levels
  • Symptoms like acne and hair changes affect body image and confidence
  • Fertility concerns can cause grief and anxiety
  • Irregular cycles create unpredictability and stress

What helps:

  • Therapy — especially CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy)
  • PCOS support communities (online and local)
  • Regular movement — exercise is proven to reduce PCOS-related anxiety
  • Sleep hygiene — poor sleep worsens insulin resistance and mood

Your feelings are valid. PCOS is a real, complex condition — you deserve proper support.

🤱 Fertility & PCOS

PCOS is the leading cause of female infertility — but most women with PCOS can and do get pregnant, often with the right support.

Why PCOS affects fertility:

  • Irregular or absent ovulation means fewer chances to conceive
  • Hormonal imbalances can affect egg quality

Options that help:

  • Lifestyle changes — weight loss can restore ovulation naturally
  • Letrozole or Clomid — medications that stimulate ovulation
  • Metformin — can improve ovulation in some women
  • IUI (intrauterine insemination)
  • IVF — effective for many women with PCOS

Talk to a reproductive endocrinologist — they specialise in exactly this.

🔬 Understanding Your Lab Results

PCOS is diagnosed using the Rotterdam Criteria — you need at least 2 of these 3 signs:

  • Irregular or absent periods
  • High androgen levels (blood test or symptoms like acne/hair growth)
  • Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound

Common tests and what they mean:

  • LH/FSH ratio — in PCOS, LH is often higher than FSH (normal ratio is 1:1)
  • Total & free testosterone — elevated in many PCOS cases
  • DHEA-S — another androgen; high levels suggest PCOS
  • Fasting insulin & glucose — checks for insulin resistance
  • AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone) — often elevated in PCOS
  • Thyroid (TSH) — ruled out as thyroid issues mimic PCOS

🏃‍♀️ Best Exercise for PCOS

Exercise is one of the most evidence-backed ways to improve PCOS symptoms — it reduces insulin resistance, lowers androgens, and lifts mood.

Best types of exercise for PCOS:

  • Strength training (2–3x/week) — most effective for insulin sensitivity
  • Walking (30 min/day) — gentle but powerful for blood sugar control
  • HIIT (1–2x/week max) — effective but don't overdo it; too much raises cortisol
  • Yoga — reduces cortisol and improves hormonal balance
  • Swimming — low-impact, great for inflammation

What to avoid:

  • Over-exercising — raises cortisol which worsens PCOS
  • Extreme calorie restriction + intense cardio — stresses the body

Start with 30 minutes of walking daily — it genuinely makes a difference.

Separating PCOS fact from fiction — because you deserve the truth. 💪


❌ Myth
"You can't get pregnant if you have PCOS."
✅ Fact
Most women with PCOS can get pregnant — often with lifestyle changes alone. Many others conceive with medications like Letrozole or through IVF. PCOS is the most treatable cause of infertility.
❌ Myth
"PCOS only affects overweight women."
✅ Fact
About 20% of women with PCOS are lean. You can have normal or low body weight and still have PCOS. Weight is not a diagnostic criterion — hormones and symptoms are.
❌ Myth
"If you have regular periods, you don't have PCOS."
✅ Fact
Some women with PCOS have regular periods but still meet diagnostic criteria through elevated androgens and polycystic ovaries. This is called "ovulatory PCOS."
❌ Myth
"Birth control pills cure PCOS."
✅ Fact
The pill manages symptoms like irregular periods and acne, but it does not treat the underlying hormonal condition. PCOS symptoms often return when you stop taking it.
❌ Myth
"PCOS is just a period problem."
✅ Fact
PCOS is a full-body hormonal and metabolic condition. It affects skin, hair, weight, mental health, insulin response, heart health, and fertility — not just your cycle.
❌ Myth
"There's nothing you can do about PCOS."
✅ Fact
While there's no cure, PCOS is highly manageable. Diet, exercise, stress reduction, and the right medications can dramatically reduce symptoms and improve quality of life.
